Private AI

Privacy is not the product; capability is the product

A short essay on private AI, sensitive context, trusted execution, and why privacy only matters when it unlocks useful work.

[ RAW MD ] [ DOWNLOAD ]
private AI AI privacy capability

Privacy is rarely the reason someone adopts an AI product. The reason is capability. People want the product to do useful work that was previously too slow, too fragmented, too risky, or too personal to hand to a generic system.

Private AI becomes compelling when it unlocks work that needs sensitive context. That can mean local files, account state, messages, health data, code, business plans, customer conversations, or internal workflows. The privacy layer matters because it makes the capability acceptable.

The user buys the workflow

A useful private AI product should answer a concrete question: what can the model do now that it could not safely do before?

If the answer is only "your data is private," the product is weak. If the answer is "the agent can use sensitive context to complete important work without leaking it," the privacy story becomes part of the value.

Trust needs a visible interface

Trust is not a paragraph in a policy. It needs to be visible inside the workflow. Users need to know what context is being used, where it is running, what can be exported, what remains local, and where the human can intervene.

For AI agents, trust is strongest when the control surface is close to the action: review the context, approve the next step, copy the output, export the artifact, stop the workflow, or keep a note private.

Timing, taste, and override

Publishing AI-generated or AI-assisted work requires judgment. Timing asks whether the work should be posted now. Taste asks whether the artifact is actually worth sharing. Override asks whether the person can change the model's path without breaking the system.

Those mechanisms matter more than a giant automation promise.
